# Literature Review — Version 0.1

**Project:** Artificial Consciousness Project  
**Author:** Bhavya Joshi  
**Date:** 17 July 2026  
**Scope:** Research foundations relevant to Organism-0, artificial selfhood, and identity continuity

---

## Purpose

This review documents the main research traditions that precede the project. It is not exhaustive. Its purpose is to prevent unsupported originality claims, identify reusable methods, and define where the project's experimental contribution may begin.

The central conclusion is that the ingredients proposed for Organism-0 already have substantial precedents. The open opportunity is not to claim invention of homeostasis, embodiment, self-modelling, autobiographical memory, or global integration. It is to combine these foundations in a transparent intervention platform focused specifically on artificial identity continuity.

## 1. Continuous body self-modelling

### Bongard, Zykov, and Lipson — 2006

**Paper:** “Resilient Machines Through Continuous Self-Modeling”  
**Published:** 17 November 2006  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1126/science.1133687

The researchers built a four-legged robot that inferred aspects of its own physical structure through actuation and sensation. After part of a leg was removed, the robot revised its internal model and discovered an alternative gait.

### Relevance

This work established that a machine can use an internal body model to adapt after unexpected structural change. Organism-0's future body-perturbation experiments must therefore be framed as replication and extension, not as a new idea.

### Remaining gap for this project

The 2006 work focused on locomotor recovery. Our later question concerns how body-model change interacts with autobiographical memory, identity attribution, and continuity across bodies.

## 2. Global-workspace cognitive architectures

### Franklin and collaborators — LIDA

**Early publication:** 2007 AAAI Fall Symposium paper  
**Paper:** “LIDA: A Computational Model of Global Workspace Theory and Developmental Learning”  
**Public record:** https://aaai.org/papers/0011-fs07-01-011-%EF%80%A0lida-a-computational-model-of-global-workspace-theory-and-developmental-learning/

LIDA implemented a working cognitive architecture inspired by Global Workspace Theory. Its modules included perception, episodic memory, action selection, procedural memory, learning, and global information broadcasting.

### Dossa and collaborators — 2024

**Paper:** “Design and Evaluation of a Global Workspace Agent Embodied in a Realistic Multimodal Environment”  
**Published:** 14 June 2024  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fncom.2024.1352685

This study trained an embodied agent navigating realistic three-dimensional environments from audiovisual inputs. It compared a global-workspace architecture with a standard recurrent architecture and found performance and robustness benefits under restricted working-memory capacity.

### Relevance

Global broadcasting and recurrent integration are established machine-consciousness research directions. The Artificial Consciousness Project should use them as testable architectural components, not assume they uniquely generate consciousness.

### Remaining gap for this project

The proposed contribution is to test whether global integration has a selective causal role in identity continuity under body, memory, and self-model conflict.

## 3. Homeostatic reinforcement learning

### Keramati and Gutkin — 2014

**Paper:** “Homeostatic Reinforcement Learning for Integrating Reward Collection and Physiological Stability”  
**Published:** 2 December 2014  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.7554/eLife.04811

The authors formalised reward as the predicted reduction of deviation from physiological target states. Their framework linked learned reward-seeking behaviour to maintenance of homeostatic stability.

### Relevance

This is a direct mathematical precedent for Organism-0's internal variables and homeostatic valuation. The project should reproduce and carefully document its chosen implementation rather than describe homeostatic reward as a new invention.

### Remaining gap for this project

Our later experiments will investigate whether homeostatic variables become part of a stable self-model and whether corrupted interoception changes identity-related attribution.

## 4. Autobiographical memory and robot selfhood

### Pointeau and Dominey — 2017

**Paper:** “The Role of Autobiographical Memory in the Development of a Robot Self”  
**Published:** 20 June 2017  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fnbot.2017.00027

The iCub research programme implemented an autobiographical memory containing episodic records and consolidated semantic knowledge. The robot accumulated 18,838 memory instances between November 2012 and May 2016. The authors connected this memory architecture to ecological, interpersonal, conceptual, and temporally extended forms of self.

### Relevance

Autobiographical memory as a foundation for artificial selfhood is an established research direction. Organism-1 must improve on simple event storage by adding memory provenance, exact checkpointing, transplantation, conflict detection, and causal lesion studies.

### Remaining gap for this project

The proposed benchmark asks what happens when autobiographical memory conflicts with body state, current capabilities, or imported histories.

## 5. Vulnerable and homeostatic feeling machines

### Man and Damasio — 2019

**Paper:** “Homeostasis and Soft Robotics in the Design of Feeling Machines”  
**Published:** 9 October 2019  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/s42256-019-0103-7

The authors argued that machines whose continued functioning depends on maintaining vulnerable internal conditions could gain intrinsic motivation and processes analogous to feelings. They proposed homeostatically regulated machines as platforms for investigating intelligence, feeling, and consciousness.

### Relevance

This is a direct conceptual foundation for the project's idea that an organism's condition must causally matter to its actions.

### Remaining gap for this project

The paper is primarily a perspective. Our contribution must come from runnable implementations, controls, causal interventions, and falsifiable measurements.

## 6. Affective artificial-consciousness programme

### Solms-led University of Cape Town project — publicly described in 2023

**Public description:** “The Matter of Consciousness”  
**Published:** 22 February 2023  
**Source:** https://neuroscience.uct.ac.za/articles/2023-02-22-matter-consciousness

The project described an artificial agent with three conflicting need categories:

- finding energy resources;
- avoiding structural damage; and
- recuperating through rest.

The agent is intended to learn by trial and error and later counterfactual planning in uncertain environments.

### Relevance

This publicly described design strongly overlaps with Organism-0's energy, structural integrity, and fatigue variables. Organism-0 must therefore be explicitly classified as a replication and engineering baseline.

### Public-evidence limitation

The public source confirms the research programme and proof-of-concept funding. This review does not assume unpublished implementation status or unpublished results.

## 7. Theory-derived indicators of AI consciousness

### Butlin and collaborators — 2023

**Report:** “Consciousness in Artificial Intelligence: Insights from the Science of Consciousness”  
**Published:** 17 August 2023  
**Preprint:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708

The authors derived computational indicator properties from several theories, including recurrent processing, global workspace, higher-order theories, predictive processing, and attention schema theory. They argued for evaluating AI systems through architectural and functional evidence rather than verbal claims.

### Relevance

The Artificial Consciousness Project should report separate theory-related measurements and avoid a single unsupported consciousness score.

### Remaining gap for this project

The proposed platform can translate selected indicator properties into executable embodied interventions and ablation tests.

## 8. Probing learned self and world representations

### Immertreu, Schilling, Maier, and Krauss — 2025

**Paper:** “Probing for Consciousness in Machines”  
**Published:** 20 August 2025  
**DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3389/frai.2025.1610225

The authors trained reinforcement-learning agents in virtual environments and used probes on internal activations to test for representations associated with the agent's position. They framed these representations as rudimentary precursors to self and world models while explicitly acknowledging that they are not sufficient for consciousness.

### Relevance

This provides a methodological precedent for probing internal representations rather than relying solely on behaviour.

### Remaining gap for this project

The study itself identifies a need to distinguish internal self models from world models and notes limitations in interoception, affective feedback, and autobiographical memory. Those limitations align directly with our revised programme.

## 9. Persistent identity in long-horizon agents

Recent preprints have begun explicitly addressing narrative continuity, persistent identity, memory-based coherence, and governance of self-modifying agents. Examples include:

- “The Narrative Continuity Test” (2025): https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.24831
- “Sophia: A Persistent Agent Framework of Artificial Life” (2025): https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.18202
- “ID-RAG: Identity Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Long-Horizon Persona Coherence” (2025): https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.25299
- “Layered Mutability: Continuity and Governance in Persistent Self-Modifying Agents” (2026): https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14717

These works show that artificial identity is becoming an active field. Much of this recent work focuses on language-model agents, narrative coherence, persona stability, or governance.

### Relevance

The project cannot claim that identity continuity itself is unstudied.

### Remaining gap for this project

Our proposed distinction is an embodied, modular, causal benchmark in which body, interoception, memory, self-model, and copying can be independently manipulated under deterministic conditions.

## 10. Consolidated conclusions

### Established before this project

- homeostatic reward and internal need regulation;
- vulnerable-machine proposals;
- robot body self-modelling;
- adaptation after structural damage;
- autobiographical robot memory;
- global-workspace architectures;
- theory-derived AI-consciousness indicators;
- probing internal self/world representations; and
- persistent identity frameworks for long-running agents.

### Not established by this review as a widely adopted standard

- an open embodied benchmark centred on identity continuity;
- exact deterministic cloning followed by controlled divergence;
- systematic body–memory conflict experiments;
- memory transplantation with provenance tracking;
- combined self-model, autobiographical-memory, and interoceptive lesions;
- a modular causal profile of which components support which continuity functions; and
- a reproducible platform joining these interventions under one interface.

The final group defines the project's provisional research gap. It must be revised if stronger prior work is found.

## 11. Citation and originality policy

1. Every publication will distinguish reproduction, integration, extension, and original contribution.
2. Prior systems will be cited before describing similar architecture.
3. Publicly unavailable work will not be treated as either completed or absent.
4. Negative literature findings will be stated as “not found in this review,” not “does not exist.”
5. This review will be updated before major claims, releases, or papers.
