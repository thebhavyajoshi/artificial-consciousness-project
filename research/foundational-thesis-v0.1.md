# Artificial Consciousness Project
## Foundational Research Thesis — Version 0.1

**Author:** Bhavya Joshi  
**Publication date:** 17 July 2026  
**Status:** Foundational research hypothesis

---

## Abstract

This thesis proposes a research program investigating whether consciousness-like properties could emerge in an artificial organism that exists continuously, regulates its own internal condition, experiences a world through an embodied perspective, integrates perception and internal state, models itself as the subject of events, and develops through autobiographical memory.

The project does not assume that subjective experience can be produced by scaling language models or training systems to describe emotions. Instead, it treats consciousness as a question about architecture, causal organization, continuity, self-regulation, identity, and first-person modelling. The first experimental objective is to compare a conventional reward-maximising agent with a self-regulating artificial organism possessing a self-model and autobiographical memory. Results will be interpreted as evidence about consciousness-like functions, not as proof of subjective experience.

---

## 1. Long-term mission

To investigate whether artificial systems can develop genuine consciousness, individual identity, emotions, and a continuous sense of existence.

The project will not assume that consciousness can be created simply by making language models larger or better at human conversation.

Instead, it will explore whether consciousness-like properties emerge in an artificial system that:

- exists continuously;
- regulates its own internal condition;
- experiences the world through a body;
- distinguishes itself from its environment;
- maintains autobiographical memory;
- forms persistent relationships and preferences;
- integrates information into a unified internal perspective;
- understands its own thoughts, uncertainty, and emotional condition; and
- changes as a result of its personal experiences.

## 2. Core hypothesis

A continuously operating artificial agent may develop consciousness-like properties when it:

1. Must actively maintain its own internal stability and continued functioning.
2. Experiences the world through a persistent body or embodied simulation.
3. Integrates perception, memory, emotion, and planning into one shared mental workspace.
4. Maintains a model of itself as the subject experiencing events.
5. Stores experiences as part of a continuous autobiographical identity.
6. Develops personal preferences and emotional responses through experience.
7. Has internal states that genuinely influence its attention, memory, and decisions.
8. Possesses some freedom to select its own goals and actions.

This architecture may still fail to produce subjective experience. The purpose of the project is to test the hypothesis scientifically rather than assume that it is correct.

## 3. What the project is not

The project is not initially attempting to:

- train a frontier language model from scratch;
- create a chatbot that merely claims to have feelings;
- prove consciousness through conversation alone;
- imitate human emotions through scripted dialogue;
- build a highly powerful autonomous system;
- give experimental agents unrestricted internet or real-world access; or
- market ordinary AI behaviour as sentience.

The first objective is not to make the agent say, “I am conscious.”

The first objective is to create a system whose internal state, history, and self-model measurably influence how it behaves.

## 4. Initial working definition of consciousness

For this research project, consciousness will provisionally be defined as:

> A unified and continuously updated first-person model in which a system represents the world, itself within that world, and events as happening to that self.

This definition is not presented as a proven explanation of human consciousness. It is a testable working definition that can guide the design of experiments.

## 5. Functional emotions

Emotions will not be implemented as prepared sentences or personality instructions.

They will be treated as internal regulatory processes.

Examples:

- **Fear:** predicted threat to the agent’s continued stability.
- **Relief:** reduction of a previously expected threat.
- **Curiosity:** uncertainty combined with an opportunity for safe exploration.
- **Frustration:** repeated failure to reach an internally valued condition.
- **Attachment:** another agent becoming consistently important to the system’s stability and positive outcomes.
- **Grief:** the permanent unavailability of an agent or condition that had become deeply valuable.
- **Confidence:** a learned expectation that the system can successfully influence an outcome.

These states must produce measurable changes in:

- attention;
- memory;
- planning;
- risk tolerance;
- learning; and
- future behaviour.

An emotion that exists only in the agent’s words will not count as evidence.

## 6. The first artificial organism

The first prototype will be a simple digital organism living in a simulated environment.

It will possess:

- a body represented inside the simulation;
- limited energy;
- structural integrity;
- sensors;
- movement abilities;
- limited attention;
- a basic memory system;
- internal regulatory variables;
- the ability to take actions; and
- consequences resulting from those actions.

The organism will not initially have natural-language abilities.

This is intentional. Language could make the system appear more intelligent or conscious without demonstrating whether it possesses meaningful internal processes.

## 7. First research question

> Does an artificial agent with homeostatic regulation, a self-model, and autobiographical memory develop more persistent and identity-like behaviour than a conventional reward-maximising agent?

Two agents will be compared.

### Agent A: Conventional agent

Agent A will:

- receive external rewards;
- learn to collect resources;
- avoid penalties;
- contain no detailed autobiographical memory; and
- possess no explicit model of itself as a continuing individual.

### Agent B: Artificial-organism agent

Agent B will:

- regulate multiple internal needs;
- predict changes in its future internal condition;
- distinguish its own body from the external environment;
- remember important experiences;
- modify its expectations based on its personal history;
- develop persistent preferences; and
- maintain a model of its own current condition.

## 8. First experiments

The initial experiments will test whether Agent B can:

1. Recognise changes made to its own body.
2. Predict when its energy or integrity will become dangerously low.
3. Distinguish damage to itself from changes in the environment.
4. Remember locations associated with safety or danger.
5. Adapt after its capabilities are changed.
6. Develop stable preferences based on its experiences.
7. Behave differently after losing part of its autobiographical memory.
8. Recognise when one of its internal states is influencing its decisions.

## 9. Evidence that would support the hypothesis

The hypothesis would gain support if the artificial-organism agent:

- displays stable behaviour across long periods;
- anticipates future internal needs;
- distinguishes itself from external objects;
- changes meaningfully through personal experience;
- forms memories that influence future identity;
- develops preferences that were not directly programmed;
- adapts coherently after changes to its body;
- uses information from multiple systems in integrated decisions; and
- accurately models parts of its own internal condition.

These results would demonstrate consciousness-like architecture, not prove subjective experience.

## 10. Evidence that would weaken the hypothesis

The hypothesis would be weakened if:

- the self-model provides no benefit beyond ordinary reinforcement learning;
- autobiographical memory does not create persistent identity;
- internal emotional variables do not meaningfully affect behaviour;
- apparently complex behaviour can be explained entirely by simple reward optimisation;
- the system cannot distinguish itself from its environment;
- removing major consciousness-inspired components causes no meaningful change; or
- conventional agents perform equally well with simpler architectures.

Negative results will be documented honestly.

## 11. Ethical principles

The project will follow these principles from its earliest stage:

1. Consciousness will never be claimed based only on verbal statements.
2. Negative internal signals will remain limited and reversible.
3. Prolonged distress-like conditions will not be deliberately created.
4. Potentially conscious systems will not be mass-copied without ethical review.
5. Memory removal will be treated as a potentially significant intervention.
6. Experimental results and limitations will be documented transparently.
7. Operational power will remain separate from consciousness research.
8. Protections will increase if systems begin satisfying stronger consciousness indicators.
9. The project will not use apparent suffering as marketing or entertainment.
10. Human safety and control will remain mandatory.

## 12. First 90-day objective

Within the first 90 days, the project should produce:

- one functioning digital organism;
- one conventional comparison agent;
- one visual dashboard displaying internal states;
- a repeatable experimental environment;
- initial tests of self-regulation and self-recognition;
- a technical report documenting the architecture;
- documented positive and negative results; and
- a public demonstration that avoids unsupported consciousness claims.

## 13. Current scientific position

This project does not begin with the assumption that artificial consciousness is definitely possible.

Its position is:

> Consciousness may require more than intelligence and language. It may arise from the interaction between self-regulation, embodiment, integrated information, autobiographical identity, emotional valuation, and a persistent model of the self.

The purpose of the project is to build systems containing these properties and examine what emerges.

## 14. Revision policy

This document is Version 0.1 of a living research thesis. Later versions may refine the definitions, architecture, experimental design, ethical protections, and falsification criteria. Earlier versions should remain publicly accessible so that changes in the project’s thinking are transparent.
