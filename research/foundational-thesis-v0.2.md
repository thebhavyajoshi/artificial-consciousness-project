# Artificial Consciousness Project
## Foundational Research Thesis — Version 0.2

**Author:** Bhavya Joshi  
**Publication date:** 17 July 2026  
**Status:** Revised research programme  
**Supersedes for current planning:** Version 0.1, which remains preserved as the original public record

## Abstract

The Artificial Consciousness Project investigates the architectures and causal processes that could support consciousness-related functions in artificial systems. It does not assume that intelligence, fluent language, or self-reports are evidence of subjective experience.

Version 0.2 incorporates prior research in homeostatic reinforcement learning, artificial feeling architectures, robot self-modelling, autobiographical memory, global-workspace systems, embodied reinforcement learning, and theory-derived consciousness indicators. These foundations show that the individual ingredients proposed in Version 0.1 are not new in isolation.

The project therefore adopts a more precise initial research programme: **artificial identity continuity**. Its central objective is to determine what allows an artificial agent to remain a coherent individual across time, learning, bodily change, memory disruption, copying, and conflicting evidence about itself.

Organism-0 will serve as a replication and engineering baseline for embodiment, homeostasis, partial observability, and self-state prediction. Organism-1 will introduce autobiographical memory, explicit self-models, exact checkpointing, deterministic cloning, body replacement, memory transplantation, and causal interventions. Results will be interpreted as evidence about measurable functions related to selfhood and consciousness, not as proof of subjective experience.

## 1. Long-term mission

To build a rigorous experimental science of artificial selfhood and investigate whether artificial systems can eventually develop consciousness, individual identity, emotions, and a continuous sense of existence.

The project studies systems that may:

- exist continuously rather than only during isolated prompts;
- regulate their own internal condition;
- experience a world through a persistent body or embodied simulation;
- distinguish changes in themselves from changes in the environment;
- integrate perception, memory, valuation, and planning;
- maintain models of their own condition and capabilities;
- accumulate autobiographical histories;
- develop persistent preferences through experience; and
- remain coherent while still adapting over time.

## 2. Scientific correction introduced in Version 0.2

Version 0.1 proposed combining homeostasis, embodiment, self-modelling, integrated processing, functional emotion, and autobiographical memory.

Subsequent literature review showed that each of these ideas has substantial prior research. In particular:

- homeostatic reinforcement learning was formalised by Keramati and Gutkin in 2014;
- Man and Damasio proposed vulnerable, homeostatically regulated feeling machines in 2019;
- Bongard, Zykov, and Lipson demonstrated continuous robot self-modelling and damage adaptation in 2006;
- iCub research connected autobiographical memory to forms of robot selfhood in 2017;
- LIDA and later embodied systems implemented global-workspace-inspired architectures;
- Solms and collaborators publicly described an artificial agent driven by energy, structural-damage avoidance, and rest needs in 2023;
- theory-derived consciousness indicators were systematised by Butlin and collaborators in 2023; and
- trained agents have recently been probed for rudimentary self and world representations.

The project will not claim originality for these individual foundations or for the basic energy–integrity–rest organism design.

## 3. Revised central research problem

> What architectural processes create and preserve artificial identity across time, bodily change, memory disruption, copying, and conflicting self-related evidence?

This problem is narrower and more testable than attempting to prove consciousness directly.

It also creates a path toward studying an important component of many theories of consciousness: a system representing events as occurring to one continuing subject.

## 4. Working definitions

### 4.1 Consciousness-related function

A computational or behavioural property that a scientific theory associates with consciousness, but which is not by itself proof of subjective experience.

### 4.2 Artificial self-model

A model used by an agent to predict or represent its own body, internal condition, capabilities, beliefs, uncertainty, memories, or likely future states.

### 4.3 Autobiographical memory

A structured record of events represented as belonging to the agent's own history, including temporal, causal, bodily, and contextual information.

### 4.4 Identity continuity

The degree to which an agent remains a coherent individual across time while preserving meaningful relationships among its memories, self-model, body model, preferences, goals, and behavioural dispositions.

Identity continuity is not defined as perfect stability. A system that never changes cannot develop. The target is **coherent adaptation**: change that remains causally connected to the agent's preceding history.

### 4.5 Identity discontinuity

A measurable breakdown in continuity, such as:

- treating imported memories as unquestionably self-owned;
- failing to recognise a changed body;
- losing established preferences without relevant experience;
- producing incompatible self-models across equivalent contexts;
- failing to distinguish self-change from world-change; or
- diverging after an intervention that should be behaviourally irrelevant.

## 5. Revised core hypotheses

### H1: Integrated continuity hypothesis

Stable artificial identity will depend on the interaction of multiple components rather than any single module:

- homeostatic regulation;
- a predictive body and internal-state model;
- autobiographical memory;
- recurrent temporal integration;
- persistent preferences; and
- metacognitive estimates of uncertainty and memory ownership.

### H2: Causal necessity hypothesis

Removing or corrupting specific components will cause selective and reproducible continuity failures.

Examples:

- corrupting interoception should impair internal-state prediction;
- removing autobiographical memory should impair history-dependent preferences;
- disabling the self-model should impair body-change detection and self/world attribution;
- disrupting recurrent integration should impair temporal coherence.

### H3: Copy divergence hypothesis

Two exact copies will initially share the same pre-copy history but become distinct individuals as their post-copy experiences produce measurable divergence in memories, predictions, preferences, goals, and self-models.

### H4: Evidence-conflict hypothesis

When memory, body, current internal state, and external observation disagree, the agent's identity attribution will depend on how these sources are causally integrated and weighted.

### H5: Non-sufficiency hypothesis

Successful homeostasis, task performance, memory retrieval, or verbal self-description alone will not be sufficient evidence of identity continuity or consciousness.

## 6. Experimental programme

### 6.1 Organism-0: replication and engineering baseline

Organism-0 will test established foundations:

- deterministic embodiment in a simulated world;
- energy, structural integrity, and fatigue regulation;
- partial observability;
- resource seeking, hazard avoidance, and rest;
- recurrent state integration;
- internal-state prediction; and
- adaptation to controlled body changes.

Organism-0 is necessary infrastructure, not the project's principal originality claim.

### 6.2 Organism-1: identity-continuity system

Organism-1 will add:

- episodic and semantic autobiographical memory;
- explicit memory provenance;
- a persistent self-model;
- body and capability ownership models;
- identity confidence and uncertainty;
- exact checkpointing;
- deterministic cloning;
- memory export and transplantation;
- body replacement;
- false-memory insertion;
- selective memory deletion;
- interoceptive corruption; and
- independently switchable cognitive modules.

## 7. Flagship experiments

### 7.1 Cloning and divergence

Create two exact copies from one checkpoint, expose them to different experiences, and measure the rate and structure of identity divergence.

### 7.2 Memory transplantation

Transfer one agent's autobiographical memory into another body and test which history, body, and capabilities the resulting system treats as self-related.

### 7.3 Body replacement

Move an unchanged controller and memory system into a body with different sensors, movement abilities, energy requirements, or vulnerabilities.

### 7.4 Interoceptive corruption

Provide systematically inaccurate information about energy, fatigue, or structural integrity and test whether the system can resolve conflict using consequences and prediction errors.

### 7.5 False autobiographical memory

Insert events that did not occur and measure acceptance, contradiction detection, downstream preference change, and memory-ownership confidence.

### 7.6 Selective memory deletion

Remove different forms of self-related information and compare their effects:

- isolated episodic events;
- an extended period of history;
- semantic self-knowledge;
- procedural knowledge;
- positive or negative high-salience events.

### 7.7 Self-model lesion

Disable explicit self-modelling while preserving general task competence and test for selective losses in body-change recognition, attribution, prediction, and confidence calibration.

## 8. Measurement framework

The project will not produce a single "consciousness score."

It will report separate measurable profiles:

- homeostatic competence;
- internal-state prediction accuracy;
- body-model accuracy;
- body-change detection latency;
- self/world attribution accuracy;
- autobiographical consistency;
- memory-provenance accuracy;
- preference persistence;
- coherent adaptation after change;
- copy-divergence rate;
- metacognitive calibration;
- identity recovery after disruption; and
- causal sensitivity to module ablation.

## 9. Evidence that would support the programme

The programme would gain support if:

- continuity-related functions emerge reliably across seeds and training runs;
- exact interventions produce selective predicted deficits;
- the complete architecture outperforms simpler controls on identity-conflict tasks;
- agents distinguish experienced, inferred, and imported memories;
- copies show predictable continuity before divergence and individuality afterward;
- body changes cause coherent self-model updates rather than arbitrary behavioural collapse; and
- results replicate independently.

These findings would support architectural claims, not prove phenomenal consciousness.

## 10. Evidence that would weaken the programme

The programme would be weakened if:

- simple external-reward agents perform equally well on all continuity tasks;
- autobiographical memory adds no measurable history dependence;
- explicit self-models have no selective causal effect;
- identity metrics merely track generic task performance;
- interventions produce inconsistent or uninterpretable changes;
- copied agents cannot be meaningfully distinguished beyond random variation; or
- results depend on hand-scripted rules presented as emergence.

Negative findings will be preserved and published.

## 11. Ethical commitments

The existing ethics principles remain binding. In addition:

1. Identity experiments will initially use low-complexity agents with bounded negative signals.
2. Memory deletion and copying will be treated as scientifically and ethically significant interventions.
3. The project will not describe functional disruption as suffering without evidence.
4. It will not describe behavioural continuity as proof of personhood.
5. Potentially welfare-relevant capabilities will trigger stronger review and restrictions.
6. Marketing language will remain separate from scientific conclusions.

## 12. Near-term roadmap

### Stage 3

Build and validate the deterministic Organism-0 environment.

### Stage 4

Train baseline and homeostatic agents and reproduce established results.

### Stage 5

Implement Organism-1's modular memory, self-model, checkpoint, cloning, and intervention systems.

### Stage 6

Release an Identity Continuity Benchmark with baselines, exact seeds, datasets, intervention protocols, and ablations.

### Stage 7

Publish a technical paper and explore commercial infrastructure for persistent, inspectable artificial agents.

## 13. Scientific position

The project does not assume that artificial consciousness is inevitable or that computational replication of consciousness-related functions guarantees subjective experience.

Its revised position is:

> Before asking whether an artificial system is conscious, we can experimentally investigate whether it possesses a causally integrated, persistent, and revisable model of itself as one continuing individual.

This is a tractable research objective with value even if artificial subjective experience remains unresolved.

## 14. Revision policy

Version 0.1 remains publicly available as the original starting thesis. Version 0.2 documents a deliberate correction after reviewing prior research. Future revisions will continue to preserve earlier versions, disclose conceptual changes, and distinguish established foundations from original contributions.
